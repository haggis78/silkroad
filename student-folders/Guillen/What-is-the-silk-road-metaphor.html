Metaphor For the Silk Road

According to historians, The Silk Road was not a definite “road” where only silk was traded, but rather, a system of trade routes that allowed merchants from across Europe and Asia to travel on to trade whatever goods they had to offer. Not only did merchants grain and sustained wealth but also the surrounding cities, villages, town, and even kingdoms thrived on the success of trades. Many places across the Eurasian continent saw great economic growth and led to the rise and fall of kingdoms for over a thousand years. [1]

Even though that the Silk Road Era was a unique time to be alive and with the advancement of technology, development of major roads, and availability of products, it is very difficult to compare anything in the modern era (twentieth and twenty-first century). In today’s day and age, especially in the western world, the only form of trading is through currency. People in the western world also rarely “haggle” because of how fixed currency is. For example, if you go to a chain store such as Walmart or Target, it will be impossible to get a lower price by simply talking the price down or offering something in return other than currency (excluding coupons). [2]

The closest thing to the Silk Road in the modern era would have to be China’s new infrastructure plan. The plan is estimated to spend over 17 trillion (USD) by 2027. At least 157 nations and international organizations have signed up with plans to create and update current and new roads, update China’s railroad to a high-speed rail line. Even though the infrastructure plan is supported by 157 nations, those who oppose to the plan are India. The infrastructure plan is not only a way to improve the travel conditions for civilians but also a way for countries to relieve their debt. [3]

This infrastructure plan resembles the Silk Road in a way that it supports the expansion of trade. Even though during the Silk Road era, automobiles, trains, and air transportation were not a thing, this plan would improve the speed of trade by eliminating transportation/ travel time. The infrastructure plan allows for the trade of perishable products and items that require delicate care, that of which wasn’t possible during the silk road era. Products such as fruits, vegetables, exotic meats and even glass are perfect examples for what is possible in this plan because during the silk road, if a merchant wanted to trade any of the goods listed, they could only do so from a short distance to avoid spoilage. [3]
