Anonymous Chinese Monk

The Anonymous Monk was travelling from Mt. Wutai to the Nälandä Monastery in what is modern day India. As a Buddhist, this was a common trip in which the monk would travel to from temple to temple on his spiritual journey to ultimately see the face of Sakyamuni.

The monk began his pilgrimage in the year 940, and it is unclear when he ended. He prayed and strengthened his knowledge at the Wu Wei, Longxing, and Dayun temples. It is believed that the monk traces back into Chinese territory after briefly entering Tibetan temple sites, such as the Longxing Temple. On the contrary, the Wu Wei and Dayun temples are Chinese sights, hence why it is believed that the monk began in China, entered Tibet, re-entered China, and then made his way to India. However, the route that the monk took after leaving Shazou (modern Dunhuang, Ganuz) from the book is an estimation based upon the manuscripts of similar travelers on that route around the same time period.

Unfortunately, his letters have been combined within the main manuscript, which is broken into three parts. The tales of the Anonymous Monk are, therefore, not set in stone after this point. These parts of the journey appear as notes from monastic officials, who also appear within them. The monk was said to have gone to an area known as the “Radiant Light”, which is said to be somewhere in or around the Dayun Temple of Dunhuang. His last letters were written here and confined within the 17th Cave, where they remained for nine-centuries. For our map of this journey, we used the 2011 Van, Schamick Sam, et al. book Manuscripts and Travellers: The Sino-Tibetan Documents of a Tenth-Century Buddhist Pilgrim.
