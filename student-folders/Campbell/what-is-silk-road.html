What is, or was, the Silk Road?

The Silk Road(s) can be described as three different, but overlapping, phenomena:

  1. A network of overland trade routes across central Eurasia in antiquity and the Middle Ages;
  2. A nineteenth-century geographical invention;
  3. A twentieth- and twenty-first century metaphor.

2. A nineteenth-century geographical invention

In 1877, the German geographer Baron Ferdinand von Richthofen coined the term die Seidenstrasse (the Silk Road) to describe the trade routes by which Chinese silk was exported to the Mediterranean world in classical antiquity and the Middle Ages. For further detail, see the article "The Invention of the Silk Road, 1877" by Tamara Chin, published in the journal Critical Inquiry Vol. 40 (2013), pp. 194-219. Sample text: George was a merchant born in Croatia some time around the year 1200. He traveled to the Middle East several times as a young man, accompanying his family firm's expeditions selling provisions to Crusaders.

In the 1240s, seeing an opening during a period of relative peace, he resolved to find a better route for the importation of high-value commodities from Asia. Having made his way across the frontier in what is now Iran, he and his companions made their way East, visiting the court of the Great Khan, traveling around the northern edge of the Taklamakan Desert, staying for at least a year at Lop Nur due to an extended illness, and eventually reaching Chang'an in 1248, before retracing his steps on almost the same route home.

Though George's trip was a mercantile failure, he claims to have kept careful notes in writing all along the way, on the basis of which he wrote this vivid account of his travels. This brought him fame but no fortune, and he is said to have died penniless in Dubrovnik in 1263 or 1264.
